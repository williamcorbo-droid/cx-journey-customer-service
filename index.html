Stage 02

Service Hub

The customer goes here to find answers to questions or problems. They can either browse ready-made content (FAQ) or reach the support team.

🚪
Mass Market customers are more likely to abandon the FAQ
Lower digital fluency and difficulty with lengthy texts make customers feel insecure and escalate to human support. 39% give up because "the text was hard to understand."
⚙️
SuCo/HI: efficiency or nothing
These customers have high digital autonomy but expect efficient, personalized content. If the text feels generic, basic, or shallow, they feel their time is being wasted and immediately demand specialized human support.
🔧
The text informs but doesn't resolve
Across all segments, what drives customers away from self-service and toward human support is content that fails to resolve or is insufficient. Top reasons: urgency (78%), complexity (75%), and insufficient information (74%).

Stage 03

Channel Choice

The moment when the customer chooses how they want to be served. They can tap "My Tickets" to continue an existing case, or go directly to chat. Currently, there is no entry point for phone contact in the app. If the customer wants to reach us by phone, they need to look up the contact number.

📱
Chat dominates, but phone persists — especially among Mass Market
Chat is the preferred channel across all segments. The key difference is in Mass Market, where chat leads preference (60%) but phone retains relevance (37%). Older-generation and lower-income customers see the phone as the most reliable way to be heard and understood.
📊
The role of phone changes by segment
For Mass Market, it's a comfort channel — the first alternative when there's insecurity. For Super Core, it's an emergency channel: used in high-stress situations (e.g., scams, blocked accounts). For High Income, it's literally the last resort when chat fails.
🧭
Lack of guidance in choosing a channel
There is no guidance from us on which channel is best suited for each type of problem. This creates friction and suboptimal choices — the customer doesn't know that different channels work better for different situations.

Stage 04

Input Gathering

The system collects the customer's request. This can happen via free text ("type what you need") or pre-defined options. It's the moment when the customer translates their problem into words — and the quality of that translation impacts everything that follows.

🎯
Cognitive load in describing the issue
Customers with lower education levels and digital fluency struggle to describe problems via text. The cognitive load of translating a complex situation into a short message generates anxiety and imprecise descriptions.

Stage 05

Chatbot

Automated support is the first line of interaction. For simple, low emotional-stress requests, the bot works well — customers value the speed and quick triage, especially Gen Y and Z. But when the situation demands listening or technical complexity, the limitations become apparent.

✅
Works well for simple tasks
For basic queries and account adjustments, the bot is praised for its speed. Customers are willing to forgo human contact when the request is straightforward and carries low emotional weight.
💜
Falls short of the "Nubank standard"
When the interaction feels too sterile, it clashes with the warm, welcoming experience customers expect from the brand. This coldness creates friction and drives the search for a human agent.
🎯
Lack of comprehension and precision
Inaccurate automated messages are a major pain point. When the bot fails to understand the actual question, the customer receives generic responses that lead to exhaustion and frustration.
⏳
Delay in reaching human support
Customers report getting stuck in "loops" with bots before reaching a human agent. This increases stress, especially in emotionally charged situations.
😰
Empathy absent in crisis moments
In scam, fraud, or urgent failure situations, interacting with a generic bot amplifies anxiety. The customer enters "reactive mode" and needs emotional validation that the bot cannot provide.

Stage 06

Human Support

The customer reaches a human agent when self-service and/or the chatbot didn't resolve the issue, or when the situation requires special care.

💬 Chat
📞 Phone
⚡
Nubank's default channel
Chat is the go-to channel — customers seek speed, objectivity, and clear written instructions. Ideal for everyday problems and situations where they want to avoid friction.
📋
Generic responses
Copy-paste responses are one of the biggest complaints — the customer feels like they're talking to a wall.
🧩
Inability to handle more complex requests
Frustration increases when the agent can't resolve more complex situations or when the customer feels there's no autonomy to actually solve the problem.
🆘
Emergency and empathy channel
Customers call when facing emergencies (scams, fraud, blocked accounts) and need immediate emotional validation. Older generations prefer phone as a guarantee of being understood.
⏳
Bureaucracy and slowness
The phone is perceived by customers as unproductive and slow, mainly due to past experiences with other companies involving long wait times and the dreaded hold music.
🔀
Automated menus and transfers
Navigating IVR menus to reach a human is a major friction point. Constant transfers between agents increase anxiety and the feeling that the problem is being ignored.

Stage 08

Ombudsman

The last resort for customers whose problems were not resolved previously. By the time they reach this point, frustration levels are already high due to unsuccessful interactions with customer service.

🔄
Repeating the story
The biggest pain point is having to explain the same problem multiple times, to different agents, across different interactions. The lack of continuity between CX and the Ombudsman team worsens the feeling of neglect.
📭
Lack of updates
The absence of proactive updates on case progress generates anxiety — especially when deadlines are missed or communicated vaguely.
🤖
Standardized apologies
Repetitive, robotic messages give the impression that nobody is actually taking responsibility for the problem.
